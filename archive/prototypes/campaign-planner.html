The end-to-end journey · Objective → Strategy → Tactics · built on the workshop frameworks

From an issue to an actionable campaign

What it is: a campaign-planning system that takes one real issue — the Leicester school street — through sense-making, objective, power analysis, pressure, strategy, tactics, organising, and resources, to an exportable plan. How it uses an agent factory: nine specialist agents (strategy, power, tactics, lobbying, organising, media, digital, tech, verification) each contribute to a shared structured campaign object, disagree on the record, and stop at human approval gates. The factory drafts; the campaigner decides; the school community acts.

Objective & decision check

No campaign materials until the objective passes the check.

🤖 strategy · objective checker

Editable: the campaigner can change the decision-maker, the timeframe, and the minimum win — the whole plan downstream recomputes from this object.

⏰ Check-in formula
We want the Director of Planning, Development & Transportation (via Cllr Whittle) to confirm permanent orders TMP1029/TMP2013 for the St John the Baptist school street by the decision following the November 2026 window, even if the outcome is only one permanent street, no replication yet.
objective checker — a naive objective, rejected

“Raise awareness of road safety in Leicester”

✗ not specific no decision-maker, no decision named
✗ not measurable no done-state — unlike counting support representations filed
✗ not time-bound ignores the Nov 2026 statutory deadline, after which awareness is worthless to this decision

Minimum viable win

Support representations formally outnumber objections on the traffic team's statutory record by early Nov 2026 — the council then cannot cite objection weight to abandon the trial, even if the decision slips.

Theory of change

If parents and residents submit individual written support during the window, the formal record confirms the 59% majority. That lowers Whittle's and Soulsby's political risk of confirming, with Castle's Greens providing cross-party cover. One confirmed order becomes the replicable playbook.

Pressure & route

What makes maintaining the status quo more costly than changing course — for these decision-makers?

🤖 power · tactics · lobbying

The specialists disagree here. The product shows the disagreement and the campaigner chooses the route.

what these decision-makers respond to
Smith (officer) · defensible file: supportive representations on the statutory record, monitoring data, low legal risk
Whittle (portfolio) · scheme succeeding publicly; no ward blowback in Knighton; internal Labour comfort
Soulsby (mayor) · legacy + low-cost win; reputational risk of “council abandons children's safety” if reversal follows visible support
Cost lever · permanent camera/planter design is cheaper than daily marshals — confirming saves money; status quo has a running cost

Route A — insider: fill the statutory record

  • Officers first, then Whittle, then cross-party cover
  • 150–300 personalised support responses into the window
  • Media used for warmth (first-day-back photo), not conflict
  • Why: the council already agrees — help them say yes

Route B — public pressure: scorecard & counter

  • Public “Leicester school streets scorecard” (schemes, not people)
  • Live response counter; louder media, faster growth
  • Risk: provokes organised objections into the same window
  • Held as escalation if Route A stalls — not deleted
Specialist disagreement № 1 — the route

Tactics: the deck's league-table play wins — public comparison creates competitive pressure and momentum.

Power + lobbying: with 59% support and a supportive portfolio holder this is an insider campaign; noise recruits the opposition into the one channel that counts.

Route A default; Route B pre-built and triggered only if the objection count overtakes support at the October check-point. Scorecard ranks schemes, never councillors (verification specialist's condition).

One-to-one guide (5 beats)

  • Their story — why does the street matter to your family?
  • The stakes — trial ends; silence reads as indifference
  • The ask — one concrete rung, this week
  • Handle hesitation — listen, don't rebut
  • Commitment — named action, named date, “can I check in Friday?”

What must stay human / what breaks without tech

  • Human: the gate ask · every one-to-one · every response's personal story · the headteacher's consent and voice
  • Tech (factory earns its keep): tracking 602 families' ladder stage · who-needs-a-nudge follow-ups · window monitoring & deadline alarms · drafting role kits captains adapt

🤖 lobbying · media · digital · organising · verifier

Campaign brief · 🤖 strategy · approve before internal use

Issue, evidence (4 stated claims + 4 unknowns), objective in check-in format, decision chain, MVW, theory of change, narrative, failing signals, next 3 steps: book officer meeting · recruit first 10 captains · draft action page.

Lobbying pack · 🤖 lobbying · approve before external use

Engagement ladder: officers → Whittle → ward councillors (letter) → MP last, sparingly. Arguments ranked for this decision-maker: ① council's own “over 60%” public commitment — confirming honours their line (never weaponise the discrepancy) ② permanent design cheaper than daily marshals ③ cross-party cover ④ national precedent. Meeting-request email (5 lines, headteacher joins, September date). Objections & responses: cost → camera enforcement payback · displacement → publish monitoring data · “41% opposed” → answerable individually. If refused: statutory representation → cross-party letter → deputation to the mayor. Press last — Whittle is an ally. Never machine-generated: the relationship, the negotiating position, real signatures.

Media pack · 🤖 media · approve before external use

Angles ranked: ① first-day-back photo op ② “will it survive?” decision-window story ③ 59/60 discrepancy — held as corrected footnote, campaign initiates the correction or doesn't touch it (disagreement № 3, verifier vs media vs lobbying: resolved as “never lead with it”) ④ marshal-cost angle — opponents get there first, pre-empt with the cheaper-permanent-design fact. Pitch email (story unclaimed — no existing coverage, verified). Quotes: headteacher-voice and parent-voice drafts, both flagged “requires the named person's consent — never auto-attributed”. Hostile Q&A: displacement · “rigged consultation” · “who pays for marshals forever”. Never: fabricate journalist contacts; imply coverage that doesn't exist.

Digital pack · 🤖 digital · approve · responses never auto-sent

Action page: one action — “Email the traffic team” — pre-drafted body citing TMP1029/TMP2013, with required personalisation fields (your street, your child's journey, one thing you've seen at the gate) because officers must weigh material individual points and discount template floods. Funnel: class WhatsApp → action page (~25–35% via trusted-parent sender) → sent response (~30–40%) → shares to 3 → volunteer list; ~40–70 responses per push, three pushes planned. Two WhatsApp messages + one social post in parent voice. Progress mechanic: gate thermometer + page counter. Email subjects: launch / halfway (“140 families have written. Has yours?”) / final week.

Organising pack · 🤖 organising · scripts approved · safeguarding checked

Volunteer roles: school-gate captain (1/year group) · street rep (own street, especially the 41%) · response-party host · coalition liaison. One-to-one guide (5 beats, above). Response-party plan: 45 minutes, kitchen table or school hall, write together, personal stories only. Coalition list: PTA, governors, cycling groups, both residents' associations. Safeguarding rule (verifier): no strangers with scripts at school gates — captains are known parents; the headteacher consents to every use of the school's name.

About this prototype

The frameworks it's built on

From the two workshop decks: Objective / Strategy / Tactics as the spine · three stages (sense-making → tactics & pressure → organising people) · the check-in formula “We want [decision-maker] to [specific action] by [time], even if the outcome is only [limited win]” · 5 whys · SMART · minimum viable win · theory of change · power mapping, pillars of power, stakeholder & resource mapping · pressure = making the status quo costlier than change · ladders of engagement, relational organising, one-to-ones, distributed organising · “It just fails — metrics, retros, iteration” · and the honest punchline “No Tech Needed” where judgement beats machinery.

How the agent factory is used — and where it deliberately isn't

Nine specialist agents (strategy, power, tactics, lobbying, organising, media, digital, campaign-tech, verification) write into one shared structured campaign object, so the objective, stakeholders, narrative, and tactics stay consistent across every asset. Their disagreements are preserved on the record (four shown inline) and resolved by the campaigner, not by consensus-mush. Deterministic code enforces the gates: SMART check before materials, stated/inferred/unknown badges, consent flags, export blocks. The factory drafts and tracks; humans hold every relationship, every send, every escalation trigger.

Known risks & limits

Real named officials appear with inferred positions — badged and quarantined from export, but still a live sensitivity for a public demo. The safeguarding rule (no strangers scripting school gates) and the never-auto-send rule are constitutional, not optional. The plan itself is a plausible first draft, not political advice: a real campaign would start by testing its assumptions at the September retro.
